Do Semantic Distance Tests Actually Predict Creativity in LLMs?

Anonymous Authors · under review at ICML 2026

Abstract

Automated semantic distance tests are increasingly used to measure the “creativity” of large language models (LLMs). However, the validity of semantic distance tests as predictors of machine creativity has not yet been established, and these tests already have limited validity as predictors of human creativity as it stands. To address this problem, we conduct the first systematic study evaluating the effectiveness of semantic distance tests in predicting the creative achievement of LLMs across three constructs: creative writing, divergent thinking, and scientific ideation. We score each test on two psychometric criteria (validity and specificity), and derive an upper bound on the maximum attainable specificity a test can achieve as a function of its validity and its construct’s correlation with general capabilities, which we refer to as the validity-specificity frontier. We find that: (1) Test effectiveness varies significantly by construct, and no single test predicts all constructs well. (2) None of the tests is a good predictor of scientific ideation ability. (3) Existing tests are far from the validity-specificity frontier, indicating meaningful room for the design of improved tests moving forward. Our findings provide clear practical takeaways and directions for future work, identifying tests which are best suited to predict each construct, and suggesting that novel tests are needed to reliably predict scientific ideation ability.
