Announcing the Creative AI Index

Samuel Schapiro·May 6, 2026

Today I’m launching the Creative AI Index — an open, community-built benchmark for measuring how creative AI models are, and for measuring the tests we use to evaluate them.

Why creativity, and why now

Most of AI’s measurable progress over the past few years has been in capabilities with sharp rubrics: math, code, reasoning. Creativity has been comparatively neglected — partly because it is hard to define, partly because it is hard to measure, and partly because the field has lacked shared infrastructure to coordinate around it.

That is starting to change. Frontier labs increasingly evaluate their models on creative tasks, but they do so privately, with proprietary rubrics on private model pools. Every claim about a model’s creativity becomes hard to reproduce, hard to compare across labs, and hard to falsify.

Creativity is going to define the next decade of AI. It deserves a shared way to measure it.

An open, shared substrate

The Creative AI Index is open-source. The leaderboard, the methodology, and every line of code that turns benchmark scores into rankings live in a public repository. Seed creativity tests are submitted by their authors via pull request, with the scoring code linked publicly. Anyone can audit, reproduce, or extend any number on the site.

This is deliberate. Creativity should not be monopolised by a small set of closed-source incumbents. Progress in this area will be best served by the open release of models, of evaluations, and of the analytical tools we use to interpret both. The Index is one piece of that — a shared substrate the community can iterate on, dispute, and improve in public.

A coordination point for the field

Creativity research in AI is currently spread across cognitive scientists, psychometricians, ML researchers, and computer scientists who each bring distinct expertise to the problem. No single venue brings these communities together on a shared object.

I would like the Index to be that venue. Somewhere a psychometrician can submit a new test and see how it compares against existing ones on the construct it claims to measure. Somewhere an ML researcher can use those results to inform what kind of training would make their next model more creative. The shared object turns “what works” from a private, lab-internal question into a public one — and one we can move on faster, together.

Get involved

The seed leaderboard covers 64 models and five creativity tests. If you have a test you’d like to add, the submission instructions are on the site, modeled on the ARC-AGI Community Leaderboard. If you maintain a model that should be on the index but isn’t, I’d love to add it.

Creativity is going to define the next decade of AI. We will get there faster, and arrive at a better place, in the open.

— Samuel Schapiro
