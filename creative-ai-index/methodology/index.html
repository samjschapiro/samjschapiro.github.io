1. Shared building blocks

Both leaderboards score models against the same set of external benchmarks, grouped into three target constructs.

Constructs and benchmarks

  • Creative Writing: Chatbot Arena Creative Writing Elo, EQ-Bench Creative Writing v3, Mazur Creative Writing v2.
  • Divergent Thinking: Hivemind diversity (1 − intra-model similarity), NoveltyBench Utility.
  • Scientific Ideation: LiveIdeaBench.

Capability stack g

g = (Arena Overall Elo, MMLU-Pro). Used by the Creativity Tests leaderboard to compute capability-controlled specificity. Arena Overall captures broad user-judged capability and MMLU-Pro captures multi-domain reasoning; controlling for both is more stringent than controlling for either alone. The Creative Achievement leaderboard ignores g — it ranks models on raw construct performance.

Canonical model pool

Both leaderboards work over the same fixed pool of models in data/canonical_models.json. Benchmark coverage is sparse — many models are scored on only a subset of the six benchmarks. Each leaderboard handles missing data differently (see below).

2. Creative Achievement (models)

Ranks models by their measured creative-achievement scores. The ranking column is a per-construct or overall mean of within-benchmark z-scores.

Why z-score

Benchmarks come in incompatible units — Arena Elo around 1500, EQ-Bench Elo around 1400–2000, NoveltyBench Utility around 3, LiveIdeaBench around 7. Averaging raw scores across benchmarks would let one benchmark dominate the others purely from its scale, not its signal. Z-scoring each benchmark across the pool first puts every benchmark on the same standardised scale before averaging.

Per-benchmark z-score

For each benchmark Y, every model M with a finite score y(M) gets:

z(M, Y) = (y(M) − meanM y) / stdM y

where mean and std are taken across the models that have a score for Y. A z of +1.00 means one standard deviation above the average pool model on that benchmark.

Per-construct and overall

For each construct C and model M, the construct score is the mean of z(M, Y)across the construct’s benchmarks Y for which M has a score. The Overall column is the mean of every per-benchmark z the model has, across all six benchmarks. We suppress the Overall score when a model has data in fewer than two constructs — otherwise a Creative-Writing-only score would masquerade as an overall ranking.

3. Creativity Tests (metrics)

Ranks tests of creativity (DAT, CDAT, PACE, …) by how well they predict the Creative Achievement benchmarks above, with extra credit for predicting them in a way not explainable by general capability.

The two criteria

Every test X is evaluated against each benchmark Y on:

  • Validity v = Pearson r(X, Y) on the intersection of models with both X and Y.
  • Specificity s = the semi-partial Pearson r between X and Y after residualising Y on g. This isolates the part of the test–benchmark association that is not explainable by general capability.

Why specificity matters

A test that simply tracks general capability will correlate with any capability-loaded benchmark — but that does not mean it measures creativity. Specificity asks: once we remove what general capability can already explain, does the test still predict the benchmark?

Theoretical ceiling

Specificity is bounded above by validity and by how much of Y is already explained by g. The covariance-PSD bound (proven in the paper’s appendix) gives:

|s| ≤ v · sqrt(1 − R²) + |R| · sqrt(1 − v²)

where R is the signed multiple correlation of Y on g. Any (v, s) pair lying above this curve is mathematically impossible. For each benchmark we compute R from the canonical pool; for each construct we take the unweighted mean of the per-benchmark ceilings as the construct-level ceiling.

Frontier point (v*, s*) and headline column

On each ceiling curve we mark (v*, s*) — the point that maximises v + s. This is the construct-attainable joint optimum: the best-possible balanced (validity, specificity) pair given how much of Y is already explained by capability. The ranking column is Frontier % = 100 × (v + s) / (v* + s*). A score of 100% means the test sits exactly at the joint optimum; lower means there is headroom; below 0% means the test anti-predicts the construct after capability control.

Sample-size guards

We require at least 5 overlapping models for any single (test, benchmark) computation. Benchmarks with fewer than 5 models on the canonical pool drop out of the ceiling computation entirely.

5. Reproducibility

All numbers on this site are computed at site build time from public files in the repository: data/canonical_models.json for the benchmark anchors, submissions/<id>/scores.json for each test. The math is implemented in lib/stats.ts (validity, specificity, ceiling, frontier optimum) and lib/models.ts (z-scoring, construct means); both are auditable directly.
